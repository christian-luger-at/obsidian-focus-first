# Daily-planning methods

A matrix tells you *what deserves attention*. A daily-planning method turns that into **today's short, ordered list** - so you start the day knowing exactly where to put your hands, and you don't drown in an endless backlog.

They all share one insight: **less is more**. Deliberately choosing a *small* number of tasks, in order, beats a long list you graze at random.

## Eat the Frog

> "Eat a live frog first thing in the morning and nothing worse will happen to you the rest of the day." - Mark Twain, popularized by Brian Tracy

Your **"frog"** is your single most important (and usually most dreaded) task. **Do it first**, before email, before anything easier. Once it's done, the rest of the day is downhill and you've already won.

- **Great for:** procrastination on one big, looming task.
- **The rule:** identify the one task that matters most, and do it first.

## The Ivy Lee method

A 100-year-old routine that still works:

1. At the **end of each day**, write down the **six** most important things to do tomorrow.
2. Put them **in priority order**.
3. Tomorrow, work **strictly top to bottom** - finish #1 before you touch #2.
4. Move anything unfinished to the next day's list.

- **Great for:** a steady, focused workday without decision fatigue.
- **The rule:** at most six tasks, ordered, one at a time.

## MITs (Most Important Tasks)

Each morning, pick **1–3 Most Important Tasks**. If you accomplish nothing else today, accomplishing these makes it a good day. Everything else is a bonus.

- **Great for:** busy days full of interruptions, when you need a floor of meaningful progress.
- **The rule:** 1–3 tasks that define a successful day.

## In Focus First

The **[Focus section](/guide/focus-size-filters#focus-tasks-today-plan)** above the matrix is built for exactly this. Tag a task `#focus` to add it, and it appears in an **ordered, numbered shortlist** - most important first.

- **#1 is your frog** - the top of the list is the first thing to eat.
- **Set a daily target** to match your method: **6** for Ivy Lee, **3** for MITs. A subtle line marks where the list runs past your target, so you can see when you've over-committed.
- **Drag to reorder** - the order is remembered, so you decide the exact sequence to work top-down.

Pick whichever method fits your day; Focus First runs all of them from the same short list.
